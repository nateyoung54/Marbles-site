## Homepage

### Hero Background
**Current file:** CSS gradient (no photo)
**Shot:** Wide-angle shot of the shop floor with CNC machines running, dramatic overhead or side lighting, slight motion blur on a machine in operation
**Why:** The hero is the first impression for every visitor — an active shop floor immediately proves the "still building" promise without saying a word.

### Navigation Panel 1 — Products
**Current file:** `Ruger Pistol.jpg` (reused elsewhere)
**Shot:** Tight macro of a finished front or rear sight against a dark, out-of-focus background — sharp metal edges and clean finish in full detail
**Why:** The Products panel needs to communicate precision and quality at a glance, which a close-up machined part does better than a full firearm.

### Navigation Panel 2 — Culture & History
**Current file:** `FactoryWorkers.jpg` (reused elsewhere)
**Shot:** Overhead or slightly elevated shot of workers at their stations on the shop floor, showing the full team environment
**Why:** The Culture panel is about people and heritage — an aerial shop floor shot makes the team and workspace the subject, not the equipment.

### Navigation Panel 3 — OEM & Manufacturing
**Current file:** `Front Sight.jpg` (reused elsewhere)
**Shot:** CNC spindle engaged in cutting steel — close enough to see the toolpath, chips, and coolant mid-operation
**Why:** OEM buyers want to see active production capability, and a machine cutting metal is more convincing than a finished part.

### Culture Section — Team Photo
**Current file:** `Marbles Employees.png`
**Shot:** Group photo of the full team or a large portion of it, either in front of the building exterior or lined up on the shop floor
**Why:** This section sells Marble Arms as a place worth working — a real, organized team photo builds that credibility.

## History Page — Timeline Images
*All 8 of these are placeholders with no image yet. These should be roughly square-cropped.*

### 1892 — Founded in Gladstone, Michigan
**Shot:** Photograph an original Universal Rifle Sight or early Marble Arms product from the museum collection, flat-lay on a neutral surface
**Why:** A physical artifact from the founding era is the most honest representation of where the company started.

### 1898 — National Recognition
**Shot:** Photograph the original Marble Game Getter, safety axe, or a framed patent document from the museum collection
**Why:** Webster Marble's 50+ patents are the story here — an original product or patent certificate makes that concrete.

### 1917 — Supporting the War Effort
**Shot:** A WWI-era military sight component, contract document, or framed historical photo from the company archive, flat-lay on aged wood or neutral surface
**Why:** Historical documents or period artifacts carry an authenticity that no staged shot can match for a wartime milestone.

### 1942 — World War II Production
**Shot:** WWII-era production parts, a stamped/dated order form, or a vintage production photograph from the company archive
**Why:** The wartime production story needs physical evidence — a dated artifact or archival photo makes the scale of the commitment tangible.

### 1968 — Focus Sharpens
**Shot:** A Uni-Ramp front sight from that era (or equivalent period part) isolated on a clean dark or wood surface, well-lit to highlight the machining
**Why:** This entry is about the product that defined the company's identity — the part itself is the most direct illustration of that focus.

### 1995 — CNC Comes to Gladstone
**Shot:** The CNC machines themselves — wide enough to show multiple axes, or a close-up of a part being cut with visible tooling engaged
**Why:** This milestone is about a specific technology arriving; showing that technology in action is the most literal and compelling representation.

### 2008 — OEM Program Launches
**Shot:** A tray or organized grouping of identical machined components ready for shipment, or a production run of the same part lined up on a surface plate
**Why:** The OEM launch is about scale and repeatability — rows of identical precision parts communicate both in a single image.

### Today — Still Here. Still Building.
**Shot:** Wide shop floor shot with multiple workers visible, machines running, bright and modern — a sense of a full operation in progress
**Why:** The final timeline entry should feel present and alive; an active, populated shop floor is the visual proof of "still building."

## Consumer Products Page

### Front Sights
**Current file:** `Front Sight.jpg`
**Shot:** 2–3 front sight models lined up side by side on a dark metal or slate surface, lit from the side to highlight the steel finish and any fiber optic elements
**Why:** Buyers comparing models need to see the product clearly — a clean multi-variant shot lets them evaluate the lineup at once.

### Rear Sights
**Current file:** `handgun and closeup sight.jpg`
**Shot:** A rear sight installed on a pistol slide, photographed from just behind the sight looking forward — the sight notch in sharp focus, barrel softly blurred ahead
**Why:** The shooter's-eye-view shows the product in its actual use context and immediately communicates what a "crisp sight picture" means.

### Peep Sights
**Current file:** `Ruger Pistol.jpg` (a pistol — does not match the product)
**Shot:** A peep tang sight mounted on a lever-action or bolt-action rifle, photographed at a 3/4 angle showing both the sight and the rifle stock it mounts to
**Why:** Peep sights are specifically for rifles — the product needs to be shown on the correct platform to reach the right buyer.

### Shotgun Sights
**Current file:** `Old Product.jpg` (generic vintage product)
**Shot:** Close-up of a fiber optic bead sight on a shotgun barrel, the bead sharp and the barrel extending to soft focus, warm natural light
**Why:** A shotgun sight is a small, subtle product — a tight barrel shot puts the bead front and center so it doesn't get lost in a wider scene.

### Accessories
**Current file:** `Accessories.png`
**Shot:** Flat-lay of the full accessory range — fiber rods, height gauge, installation tools, hardware — arranged neatly on a dark steel or slate surface
**Why:** Accessories are a supporting product family; showing the full range together communicates completeness and helps buyers discover items they didn't know existed.

## General Tips

- **Aspect ratio:** Most section images on the site are landscape (wider than tall) at roughly 16:9 or 3:2. Timeline images should be square or close to it (1:1).
- **Lighting:** Side lighting from a window or directed light source will show machined surface texture far better than flat overhead fluorescent. Bring a portable LED panel if the shop lighting is flat.
- **Background:** Dark surfaces (slate, steel, black foam board) work best for individual product shots given the site's dark aesthetic. The product will pop without looking overproduced.
- **Depth of field:** For product shots, a shallow depth of field (blurred background) looks natural and premium. For shop floor/team shots, keep more in focus so the environment reads clearly.
- **File naming:** Keep filenames short, lowercase, no spaces — e.g., `front-sight-lineup.jpg`, `cnc-1995.jpg`, `team-shop-floor.jpg`.
